Want to understand these metrics?
Walking/running gait, common issues, and fixes are now in the Info sub-tab.

What good walking looks like: heel contacts first (lateral side), weight rolls smoothly through the midfoot, toes push off last. Stride length matches your height — don't over-reach. Cadence 100–125 spm for moderate pace.

Signals on this app: S1 (heel) peaks first, S2 (midfoot) next, S3 (forefoot) last — each cleanly separated. L/R load share stays close to 50/50 over a session.

Double-support: walkers always have at least one foot on the ground. The stance phase overlap with the other foot is ~10% of cycle per step.

What good running looks like: foot lands roughly under the center of mass — usually midfoot, sometimes light forefoot — not out in front of you. Quick, springy contact (200–240 ms). Both feet leave the ground between steps — that's the flight phase that distinguishes running from walking.

Signals on this app: S2 and S3 peak together (or S3 slightly later). Cadence 170–185 spm. Vertical oscillation < 10 cm; vertical ratio < 7%.

Efficient cue: "run tall, run quiet." Tall posture, short ground contact, minimal bounce. Forward drive from hip extension, not from pushing the knee forward.

Over-striding
Foot lands well ahead of your center of mass — common with heel striking at speed. Acts as a braking force every step; elevates knee and tibial stress. Fix: Increase cadence 5–10%; aim to land under your hip.
Hip drop (Trendelenburg)
When one leg is in stance, the opposite hip drops instead of staying level. Signals weak hip abductors on the stance side. Common cause of ITB syndrome and knee pain. Fix: Side-planks, single-leg squats, banded clamshells.
Excessive vertical oscillation
Bounce > 10 cm wastes energy going up instead of forward. Usually pairs with low cadence and long ground contact. Fix: Increase cadence toward 175–180 spm; cue "run quiet, land light."
Over-pronation
Arch collapses inward on landing. S2 (midfoot) sensor sees disproportionate load relative to S1/S3. Can contribute to plantar fasciitis, shin splints, bunions. Fix: Single-leg balance on a cushion, toe-yoga, stability shoes or orthotics if symptomatic.
Asymmetric strike / stance time
One foot lands differently — or stays on the ground noticeably longer — than the other. Usually a compensation from a past injury or leg-length difference. Fix: Unilateral strength work on the weaker side; if persistent for weeks, a physio check and possibly a video gait analysis.
Note: these are pattern descriptions, not medical diagnoses. If you have pain that persists, consult a sports physiotherapist or podiatrist.

Cadence · steps per minute

Steps per minute combining both feet. Higher cadence distributes the same distance across more, smaller steps — which reduces impact per step and usually improves efficiency.

Targets: casual stroll 85–95, brisk walk 115–125, recreational running 160–170, efficient running 175–185, elite / sprint 190+.

How we measure it: from pressure-sensor step detection (no wrist or pod needed). One step = one foot landing.

Stride length

Stride = distance between two successive contacts of the same foot (L to L, or R to R). A step is half a stride (L to R). Stride length scales with height and speed.

How it's computed here: GPS speed × time between same-foot strikes (detected from pressure). Typical stride: walking 1.2–1.6 m, recreational running 2.0–2.4 m, fast running 2.6–3.2 m.

Over-striding is when stride grows faster than cadence as you speed up — foot lands ahead of you and brakes. The fix is to increase cadence rather than reach.

Vertical oscillation & vertical ratio

Vertical oscillation (VO) is the amount your center-of-mass moves up and down per step — the "bounce." Typical running: 6–10 cm. Lower is usually better; you want forward motion, not vertical.

Vertical ratio (VR) = VO ÷ stride length × 100%. Normalizes bounce by how far you traveled. Elite runners sit under 6%; hobbyists commonly 7–9%; above 10% suggests mechanics to work on.

Corrective cues: "run tall, run quiet." Quicker cadence automatically lowers bounce without conscious effort.

Ground contact time (GCT) & L/R balance

GCT is the milliseconds each foot spends on the ground per step. Running: 200–280 ms is typical; elites often 180–200 ms. Walking: 500–700 ms. Shorter GCT = springier stride and less braking.

GCT balance (L/R) is the percentage split between feet. Truly even would be 50/50. Most people have natural variation of 48/52 to 52/48. Differences > 4% are worth investigating — usually strength imbalance, past injury, or leg-length discrepancy.

How we measure it: each insole's pressure sum crosses a threshold on contact and again on lift-off. The time between those events is GCT for that foot.

HR zones — what they mean

Z1 · Warm-up — very easy, "could talk in full sentences." Builds aerobic base, aids recovery.

Z2 · Easy / base — conversational pace, foundation of endurance. Most training time should live here.

Z3 · Aerobic / tempo — comfortably hard, "marathon pace" territory. Builds efficiency but with higher recovery cost.

Z4 · Threshold — hard but sustainable for 20–60 min. Lactate starts to accumulate. Used for threshold workouts.

Z5 · VO₂max / max — all-out, 2–8 min intervals. Builds peak aerobic capacity.

7-zone schemes split the top (Z4+) into Sub-threshold, VO₂max, Anaerobic, and Neuromuscular — useful if you train seriously at intensity.

%HRmax vs Karvonen (HR reserve)

%HRmax (simple method): zone bands = fraction of your maximum heart rate. Works fine for most people.

Karvonen method (more accurate): uses heart-rate reserve — the range between resting and maximum HR. Zone bands are calculated as fractions of HRR added to your resting HR:

zone threshold = HRrest + (HRmax − HRrest) × fraction

Why it matters: two people with the same HRmax but different resting HR have very different cardiac reserve. A fit athlete with rest HR 50 works much harder to reach 150 bpm than someone with rest HR 75. Karvonen accounts for that.

Set both Max HR and Resting HR in your Profile to unlock Karvonen; otherwise the app defaults to %HRmax.

HR drift

If your pace stays constant but HR slowly climbs across a session, that's cardiovascular drift. Normal causes: dehydration, heat accumulation, fatigue. Large drift (> 5–10 bpm over 30 min) suggests fueling or pacing issues.

The HR sub-tab shows drift as bpm/minute, calculated from the average HR in the first half of the session vs. the second half.

Time-in-zone & polarized training

Tracking how much session time lives in each zone reveals your training pattern. Most elite endurance athletes follow 80/20 — about 80% of time in easy Z1–Z2, 20% in hard Z4–Z5, with little in between (the "moderate" trap).

Everyday runners often flip this: too much Z3 (tempo), leading to cumulative fatigue without the peak-stress benefits of real intervals or the recovery benefits of truly easy runs.

Use the TIZ bar on the Biomech screen and the Trend card here to check whether your week looks polarized or stuck in the middle.

The three pressure zones

Each insole has three pressure sensors. With 6 total (3 per foot), we can map which part of the foot is bearing load through every step of every session.

S1 · Heel — first contact in walking and heel-striking running. Big cushioning zone.

S2 · Midfoot — carries load during midstance; reflects pronation/supination tendencies.

S3 · Forefoot — push-off zone; carries propulsion load. Activated last.

Weight distribution & what healthy looks like

Walking — roughly 30% heel / 25% midfoot / 45% forefoot over a session. Load rolls front-to-back-to-front smoothly.

Running — depends on strike pattern (below). Expect 15–25% heel, 25–35% midfoot, 45–60% forefoot.

Worth attention:

Heel > 50% of session — consider cadence work and foot-strike drills (especially if running).

Midfoot < 18% — "skipping" pattern; midfoot isn't engaging through midstance.

Forefoot > 55% — may overload calves and Achilles; stretch and add eccentric heel drops.

Left / Right balance

The L/R split shows what percentage of total load each foot carries over a session. Target is close to 50/50, but asymmetry up to ~4% is normal and individual.

4–8% — slight lean; normal for most people.

8–15% — mild asymmetry; worth addressing with unilateral strength work.

> 15% — significant; raises injury risk. If persistent over weeks, a physio or sports-med evaluation is a good idea.

Causes are usually strength imbalance, old injury compensation, leg-length difference, or stiffness on one side.

Strike pattern

Heel strikers land S1 first. Common in walking and easy-pace running. At faster paces, heel striking can be a braking force if combined with over-striding.

Midfoot strikers land S2 first with S1 and S3 closely behind. Generally considered most efficient for distance running.

Forefoot strikers land S3 first. Common at faster paces and in sprinters. Loads calves and Achilles more — more calf/Achilles strength work needed.

No strike pattern is "wrong" in isolation. What matters is whether it suits your pace, history, and has you landing under your body, not ahead of it.
